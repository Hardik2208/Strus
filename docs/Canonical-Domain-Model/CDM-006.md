1. Purpose

The Submission Domain represents the contractual evidence of work completed by professionals.

A submission is not merely a collection of uploaded files.

It represents the complete deliverable package submitted for review against a milestone.

This domain manages:

Submission lifecycle
Submission templates
Deliverables
Deliverable fields
Artifacts
Storage abstraction
Submission history
Versioning
Retention

It does not own milestone approval.

It does not own revisions.

It does not own payments.

2. Submission Philosophy

One of the biggest architectural decisions we made was:

Strus should never hardcode submission types like Development, Design or Video Editing.

Instead,

every milestone defines its own submission requirements.

This makes the platform profession-independent.

26. Business Rules
Every milestone defines exactly one SubmissionTemplate.
SubmissionTemplates generate submission forms dynamically.
Deliverables are created from DeliverableTemplates.
Fields are created from FieldTemplates.
Artifacts describe business resources.
StorageObjects describe physical storage.
Files never enter PostgreSQL.
Storage providers remain replaceable.
Every revision creates a new Submission.
Submitted artifacts become immutable.
Business metadata survives storage cleanup.
27. Golden Rules
Submission represents contractual work, not uploaded files.
Templates define structure.
Deliverables organize work.
Fields capture values.
Artifacts represent business evidence.
StorageObjects abstract storage providers.
Cloudinary stores files only.
PostgreSQL stores business records only.
Storage cleanup never removes business history.
Submission history is immutable and permanently auditable.
Architecture Observation

There is one additional entity I recommend introducing before freezing the model:

SubmissionReview

Currently, review information is implied through submission status and comments. However, your business rules include:

Who reviewed
Review started
Review completed
Approval
Revision request
Rejection reason

Rather than embedding these into Submission, model them explicitly:

Submission
    │
    └── SubmissionReview
            ├── reviewerId
            ├── decision
            ├── reviewStartedAt
            ├── reviewedAt
            ├── rejectionReason
            └── linkedRevisionRequestId

This cleanly separates the submitted work from the review process, improves auditability, and better reflects the workflow you've defined without changing any of your business rules. I would include this entity in the final canonical ER model before moving on to the Financial Domain.
